Lange v Australian Broadcasting Corporation

(1997) 189 CLR 520

Background

Former New Zealand Prime Minister David Lange sued the ABC for defamation over a documentary that examined his government's relationship with big business. The ABC defended on the basis of the implied freedom of political communication established in the 1992 cases.

The case provided an opportunity for the High Court to reconsider the constitutional defence to defamation established in the 1994 cases of Theophanous and Stephens.

High Court Decision

The High Court unanimously clarified and refined the implied freedom of political communication, establishing the framework that continues to govern constitutional analysis today.

Confirmed the existence of implied freedom of political communication
Established the two-stage test for constitutional validity
Refined the defence of qualified privilege in defamation law

Legal Impact

The Lange decision had profound implications for Australian constitutional law:

  • • Established clear framework for analyzing restrictions on political communication
  • • Clarified that the freedom is structural, not an individual right
  • • Created new form of qualified privilege in defamation law
  • • Provided foundation for subsequent constitutional litigation
Sally Walker, Lange v ABC: the High Court rethinks (1998)

Levy v Victoria

(1997) 189 CLR 579

The Duck Hunting Protest

Mr. Levy was a long-time campaigner against duck hunting who would enter hunting areas during the opening of duck season to collect injured and dead birds, particularly endangered species. He would then display these birds to media at the Victorian Parliament to protest laws allowing duck hunting.

He was charged under regulations that prohibited entry to designated duck hunting areas during specified times without a valid hunting permit. Levy argued these regulations impermissibly burdened his freedom of political communication.

Constitutional Analysis

The High Court applied the Lange test to determine whether the hunting regulations impermissibly burdened political communication:

Stage 1: Burden

Court found the regulations did burden political communication by preventing effective protest activities.

Stage 2: Justification

Regulations were found to be reasonably appropriate and adapted to ensure public safety and order.

Significance

The Levy case demonstrated several important principles:

  • • Political communication extends to protest activities
  • • Not all burdens on political communication are unconstitutional
  • • Public safety can justify restrictions on protest rights
  • • Context matters in applying the Lange test

Brown v Tasmania

(2017) 261 CLR 328

Anti-Protest Laws

Former Australian Greens leader Bob Brown challenged Tasmania's anti-protest legislation, which created new offences for protesting on business premises and provided police with expanded powers to disperse protests.

The legislation was introduced following environmental protests against logging operations and mining projects. Brown argued the laws impermissibly burdened the implied freedom of political communication.

High Court Ruling

The High Court invalidated key provisions of the Tasmanian legislation, finding they impermissibly burdened political communication. The Court emphasized several key principles:

Protest activities are protected forms of political communication
Laws targeting protest require compelling justification
Economic interests alone may not justify speech restrictions

Contemporary Relevance

The Brown decision has significant implications for modern protest rights:

  • • Reinforced protection for environmental and political protests
  • • Clarified limits on government power to restrict dissent
  • • Demonstrated ongoing vitality of the implied freedom
  • • Influenced similar challenges in other jurisdictions

Theophanous v Herald & Weekly Times

(1994) 182 CLR 104

Political Defamation Defence

Federal MP Andrew Theophanous sued the Herald & Weekly Times for defamation over a letter published in The Age newspaper that criticized his conduct in relation to immigration matters. The newspaper defended on the basis of the implied freedom of political communication.

The case raised the question of whether the Constitution provides protection for political discussion even when it involves defamatory statements about politicians.

Constitutional Defence Established

The High Court recognized a constitutional defence to defamation when the communication relates to political discussion. The Court held that:

"The Constitution impliedly requires that the powers and capacities of government be exercised in a manner which does not impair the freedom of communication between members of the community concerning political or government matters."

Subsequent Development

Although the specific constitutional defence established in Theophanous was later refined in Lange, the case was significant because it:

  • • First applied implied freedom to defamation law
  • • Recognized broad scope of political communication protection
  • • Established constitutional limits on common law
  • • Demonstrated the practical impact of implied freedoms
