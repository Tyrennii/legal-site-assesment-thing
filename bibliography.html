Glossary of Terms

Key terms and concepts in freedom of expression law

Implied Freedom of Political Communication

A constitutional principle derived from the text and structure of the Australian Constitution that protects communication about government and political matters. It operates as a limitation on legislative and executive power rather than an individual right.

Lange Test

A two-stage test established in Lange v ABC (1997) for determining whether a law impermissibly burdens the implied freedom of political communication. Stage 1 examines if a burden exists; Stage 2 assesses whether the law is reasonably appropriate and adapted to serve a legitimate end.

Proportionality Test

A legal analysis framework used to determine whether restrictions on rights are justified. Requires that limitations be suitable, necessary, and balanced in achieving legitimate governmental objectives while minimally infringing on protected rights.

Section 18C

A provision of the Racial Discrimination Act 1975 that makes it unlawful to "offend, insult, humiliate or intimidate" someone based on their race, color, or national/ethnic origin. The subject of ongoing debate about balancing anti-vilification with free speech.

Qualified Privilege

A defence in defamation law that protects statements made on occasions where there is a legal, social, or moral duty to communicate. The Lange case established a new form of qualified privilege for political communication, subject to reasonableness requirements.

Structural Protection

A constitutional protection that operates by limiting government power rather than conferring individual rights. Australia's implied freedom of political communication is a structural protection designed to ensure the effective functioning of representative government.

Vilification

Speech or conduct that incites hatred, serious contempt, or severe ridicule against a person or group based on protected characteristics such as race, religion, or sexual orientation. Australian law prohibits vilification in various jurisdictions through anti-discrimination legislation.

Chilling Effect

The phenomenon where laws or regulations that are not explicitly prohibitive may discourage the legitimate exercise of freedom of expression. Fear of legal consequences or social backlash can lead to self-censorship and reduced public discourse.

Marketplace of Ideas

A theory that the best ideas will emerge from open competition in public discourse. This concept supports broad protection for freedom of expression, suggesting that truth will prevail through rational discussion rather than censorship.

Prior Restraint

Government action that prevents speech or publication before it occurs. Prior restraint is considered a particularly serious infringement on freedom of expression and faces a high constitutional threshold for justification in many legal systems.

Public Interest

A legal standard used to determine whether speech or publication serves the broader interests of society. Public interest considerations often play a crucial role in balancing freedom of expression against other competing rights and interests.

Bill of Rights

A formal declaration of fundamental rights and freedoms that may be constitutionally entrenched or legislatively enacted. Australia lacks a comprehensive Bill of Rights at the federal level, leading to reliance on common law, legislation, and implied constitutional principles.
