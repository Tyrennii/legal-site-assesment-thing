International Framework

Understanding the global foundations of freedom of expression through international human rights law

Universal Declaration of Human Rights

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR), adopted by the United Nations General Assembly on December 10, 1948, establishes the foundational principles for freedom of expression globally. Article 19 of the UDHR explicitly protects the right to freedom of opinion and expression.

Article 19 - UDHR

"Everyone has the right to freedom of opinion and expression; this right includes freedom to hold opinions without interference and to seek, receive and impart information and ideas through any media and regardless of frontiers."
United Nations, Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)

This groundbreaking provision established three core components of freedom of expression: the right to hold opinions, the right to seek and receive information, and the right to impart information and ideas. The phrase "through any media" was particularly forward-thinking, anticipating the development of new communication technologies.

International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights

The International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR), adopted in 1966 and entering into force in 1976, transformed the principles of the UDHR into binding international law. Australia ratified the ICCPR in 1980, assuming legal obligations to protect and promote the rights it contains.

Article 19 - ICCPR

Paragraph 1: "Everyone shall have the right to hold opinions without interference."

Paragraph 2: "Everyone shall have the right to freedom of expression; this right shall include freedom to seek, receive and impart information and ideas of all kinds, regardless of frontiers, either orally, in writing or in print, in the form of art, or through any other media of his choice."

Paragraph 3: The exercise of these rights carries "special duties and responsibilities" and may be subject to restrictions that are "provided by law and are necessary" for respect of others' rights, protection of national security, public order, or public health and morals.

International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (1966)

Australia's Ratification

Australia ratified the ICCPR on August 13, 1980. However, Australia's approach to implementation has been through common law and legislation rather than constitutional entrenchment, leading to ongoing debates about the direct applicability of international human rights standards.

The Three Components of Freedom of Expression

Right to Hold Opinions

Absolute protection for internal thoughts and beliefs without external interference or coercion.

Right to Seek & Receive

Access to information from diverse sources without unreasonable restrictions or censorship.

Right to Impart

Share information and ideas through various media and communication channels.

The Human Rights Committee has emphasized that these components are interconnected and essential for meaningful participation in democratic society. The right to freedom of expression applies to information and ideas "of all kinds," regardless of their content, and extends across national boundaries.

Permissible Limitations

Article 19(3) of the ICCPR acknowledges that freedom of expression is not absolute and may be subject to certain restrictions. However, these restrictions must meet a strict three-part test:

1. Provided by Law

Restrictions must be clearly defined in domestic law, providing sufficient guidance to prevent arbitrary application.

2. Legitimate Purpose

Restrictions must pursue one of the legitimate aims specified in Article 19(3): respect for others' rights or reputations, protection of national security, public order (ordre public), public health, or public morals.

3. Necessary and Proportionate

Restrictions must be necessary and proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued. This requires demonstrating that the restriction is the least restrictive means available to achieve the desired purpose.

Proportionality Test

The Human Rights Committee has established that restrictions must be proportionate, meaning they must not be overbroad and should be carefully tailored to achieve the legitimate aim without unduly restricting the core right to freedom of expression.
